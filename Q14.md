# Question 14: Bronsted-Lowry Bases

## Unit: Unit 8 - Acids and Bases
**Topic**: Bronsted-Lowry Acid-Base Theory (SAP-8.A)

---

## Original Question
Which sentence is true of all Bronsted-Lowry bases?

A. They can donate a hydrogen ion.
B. They can accept a hydrogen ion.
C. They can produce hydronium.
D. They are positively charged.
E. They are negatively charged.

---

## How to Approach This Question

### Step 1: Recall Bronsted-Lowry Definitions

**Bronsted-Lowry Acid**: A substance that **donates** a proton (H‚Āļ ion)
**Bronsted-Lowry Base**: A substance that **accepts** a proton (H‚Āļ ion)

**Key Memory Aid:**
- **A**cid = **A**way (gives away H‚Āļ)
- **B**ase = **B**rings in (accepts H‚Āļ)

---

### Step 2: Evaluate Each Option

**A. They can donate a hydrogen ion.**
- ‚ĚĆ This is the definition of an **acid**, not a base
- Acids donate H‚Āļ

**B. They can accept a hydrogen ion.**
- ‚úÖ This is the exact definition of a Bronsted-Lowry base
- Bases accept H‚Āļ

**C. They can produce hydronium.**
- ‚ĚĆ Acids produce hydronium (H‚āÉO‚Āļ) when they donate H‚Āļ to water
- Example: HCl + H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ

**D. They are positively charged.**
- ‚ĚĆ Not all bases are positively charged
- Examples: NH‚āÉ (neutral), OH‚ĀĽ (negative), Na‚Āļ (positive but not a base)

**E. They are negatively charged.**
- ‚ĚĆ Not all bases are negatively charged
- While OH‚ĀĽ is negative, NH‚āÉ is a base but is neutral

**Answer: B (They can accept a hydrogen ion.)**

---

## Key Concepts to Remember

### Bronsted-Lowry Theory

| Type | Definition | Action | Example |
|------|------------|--------|---------|
| **Acid** | Proton donor | Gives away H‚Āļ | HCl, H‚āāSO‚āĄ, NH‚āĄ‚Āļ |
| **Base** | Proton acceptor | Accepts H‚Āļ | NH‚āÉ, OH‚ĀĽ, H‚āāO |

### Important Points:
1. **Charge doesn't determine acid/base behavior**
   - NH‚āĄ‚Āļ is an acid (can donate H‚Āļ)
   - OH‚ĀĽ is a base (can accept H‚Āļ)
   - NH‚āÉ is a base (neutral, can accept H‚Āļ)

2. **Water can be both** (amphoteric):
   - As acid: H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ
   - As base: H‚āāO + H‚Āļ ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ

3. **Conjugate acid-base pairs**:
   - When a base accepts H‚Āļ, it becomes its conjugate acid
   - When an acid donates H‚Āļ, it becomes its conjugate base

### Example Reaction:
```
NH‚āÉ  +  HCl  ‚Üí  NH‚āĄ‚Āļ  +  Cl‚ĀĽ
base   acid    conj.acid  conj.base
```

### Feedback

**Approach:**
1. Identify which species **accepts** a proton (H‚Āļ)
2. That species is the Bronsted-Lowry base
3. Track where the H‚Āļ goes

**Analysis:**

Let's track the hydrogen atoms:
- **HNO‚āÉ** starts with 1 H ‚Üí becomes **NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ** with 0 H
  - Lost H‚Āļ ‚Üí **ACID** (proton donor)

- **H‚āāO** starts with 2 H ‚Üí becomes **H‚āÉO‚Āļ** with 3 H  
  - Gained H‚Āļ ‚Üí **BASE** (proton acceptor)

**Correct Answer: B (H‚āāO)**

Water acts as a Bronsted-Lowry base in this reaction because it accepts a proton from nitric acid. The water molecule uses one of its lone pairs on oxygen to bond with the H‚Āļ, forming hydronium ion (H‚āÉO‚Āļ).

**The complete picture:**
- HNO‚āÉ = acid (donates H‚Āļ)
- H‚āāO = base (accepts H‚Āļ)
- H‚āÉO‚Āļ = conjugate acid of H‚āāO
- NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ = conjugate base of HNO‚āÉ

**Key Insight:**
This reaction shows that water can act as a base even though we often think of it as neutral. When water accepts a proton from a strong acid like HNO‚āÉ, it demonstrates basic behavior according to Bronsted-Lowry theory.
