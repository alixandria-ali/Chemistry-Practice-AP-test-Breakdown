Question 3: Sulfur Trioxide (10 points)

Units Covered

  • Unit 2: Molecular Structure (Lewis structures, VSEPR)
  • Unit 6: Thermodynamics (Enthalpy, Entropy, Gibbs Free Energy)
  • Unit 7: Equilibrium (Equilibrium expressions, Le Chatelier's Principle)

Time: ~23 minutes | Points: 10

Question Overview

Decomposition Reaction: 2 SO₃(g) → 2 SO₂(g) + O₂(g)
ΔH°rxn = +180 kJ/mol

Part (a): Lewis Structure of SO₃

Question

Complete a valid Lewis electron-dot diagram for the SO₃ molecule. Include all valence electrons, including lone pairs.

How to Approach

Step 1: Count total valence electrons - S: 6 valence electrons - O (×3): 18 valence electrons - Total: 24 valence electrons

Step 2: Draw skeleton structure with S in center

Step 3: Complete octets and check formal charges

Solution

Valid Lewis Structure (one of three resonance structures):

     O
     ‖
  O=S=O

Or with formal charges shown: - Central S has 3 double bonds to oxygen atoms - Each O has 2 lone pairs - This is one resonance structure; two others exist with the double bond on different oxygens

Alternative representation: S can have one double bond and two single bonds with formal charges.

Key Concepts

Resonance: SO₃ has three equivalent resonance structures where the double bond rotates among the three oxygen atoms.

Formal Charge = (Valence e⁻) - (Lone pair e⁻) - ½(Bonding e⁻)

Part (b): Molecular Shape

Question

Predict the shape of the SO₃ molecule based on the Lewis diagram.

How to Approach

Step 1: Count electron domains around central atom - 3 bonding domains (3 oxygen atoms) - 0 lone pairs on sulfur

Step 2: Apply VSEPR theory

Solution

Shape: TRIGONAL PLANAR

  • 3 electron domains → trigonal planar geometry
  • Bond angles: 120°
  • Molecular geometry matches electron geometry (no lone pairs on central atom)

Key Concepts

VSEPR Theory: Electron domains arrange to minimize repulsion - 2 domains → linear (180°) - 3 domains → trigonal planar (120°) - 4 domains → tetrahedral (109.5°)

Part (c): Thermodynamic Favorability

Question

Under what temperature conditions will the decomposition be thermodynamically favored: high temperatures, low temperatures, all temperatures, or no temperatures?

How to Approach

Step 1: Analyze the reaction - ΔH°rxn = +180 kJ/mol (endothermic, positive) - Products have more gas molecules than reactants → ΔS° > 0 (positive)

Step 2: Apply Gibbs Free Energy equation - ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS° - Reaction is favored when ΔG° < 0

Step 3: Determine temperature effect

Solution

HIGH TEMPERATURES ONLY

Justification: - ΔH° = +180 kJ/mol (positive, unfavorable) - ΔS° > 0 (positive, favorable) because 2 moles gas → 3 moles gas - For ΔG° to be negative: TΔS° must be greater than ΔH° - This occurs only at high temperatures where the TΔS° term becomes large enough to overcome the positive ΔH°

Key Concepts

Gibbs Free Energy: ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS°

ΔH° ΔS° Temperature Dependence
- + All temperatures (spontaneous)
+ - No temperatures (never spontaneous)
- - Low temperatures only
+ + High temperatures only

Part (d): Equilibrium Graph Analysis

Question

A rigid vessel is filled with SO₃(g) at 0.83 atm. The system reaches equilibrium, then a change is made at time t₁.

(i) Mark when the system first reached equilibrium (ii) Describe the change made at time t₁ (iii) Explain why P(SO₃) increased while P(O₂) decreased after t₁

How to Approach

Look for: - When partial pressures become constant (equilibrium) - What changes at t₁ - Direction of shift (Le Chatelier's Principle)

Solution

(i) Time of first equilibrium: Mark X approximately halfway between 0 and t₁ where all three curves level off.

(ii) Change at t₁: Additional SO₂(g) was injected into the reaction vessel. - Evidence: P(SO₂) suddenly jumps up at t₁

(iii) Explanation: The increase in P(SO₂) disturbed the equilibrium. According to Le Chatelier's Principle, the system responds by shifting to consume the added SO₂: - Reaction shifts left (reverse direction) - O₂ is consumed (P decreases) - SO₃ is produced (P increases) - System re-establishes equilibrium at new concentrations

Alternative explanation using Q: - Before t₁: Q = K (at equilibrium) - After adding SO₂: Q increases (numerator larger) - Since Q > K, reaction shifts left to decrease Q back to K - This consumes O₂ and produces SO₃

Key Concepts

Le Chatelier's Principle: When a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to relieve that stress.

Stresses: - Adding reactant → shifts right - Adding product → shifts left - Increasing temperature (endothermic) → shifts right - Decreasing volume/increasing pressure → shifts toward fewer moles of gas

Part (e): Equilibrium Constant Expression and Calculation

Question

(i) Write the Kₚ expression (ii) Calculate the value of Kₚ

How to Approach

Step 1: Write Kₚ using partial pressures - Products in numerator - Reactants in denominator - Coefficients become exponents

Step 2: Read values from graph at equilibrium (before t₁)

Solution

(i) Equilibrium Expression:

Kₚ = [P(SO₂)]² × P(O₂) / [P(SO₃)]²

(ii) Calculation:

Reading from graph at first equilibrium (before t₁): - P(SO₃) ≈ 0.50 atm - P(SO₂) ≈ 0.33 atm - P(O₂) ≈ 0.17 atm

Substitute values:

Kₚ = (0.33)² × (0.17) / (0.50)²

Kₚ = (0.1089) × (0.17) / (0.25)

Kₚ = 0.0185 / 0.25

Kₚ = 0.074

Alternative reading (slightly different graph values): If reading P(SO₃) = 0.57, P(SO₂) = 0.41, P(O₂) = 0.14:

Kₚ = (0.41)² × (0.14) / (0.57)² = 0.072

Key Concepts

Equilibrium Constant: - Kc: uses molar concentrations [M] - Kₚ: uses partial pressures (atm) - Only for gaseous species - Temperature dependent - K > 1: products favored - K < 1: reactants favored

For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kₚ = (Pᴄ)ᶜ(Pᴅ)ᵈ / (Pᴀ)ᵃ(Pʙ)ᵇ

Modified Question 3

Consider the equilibrium: N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2 NO₂(g) ΔH° = +58 kJ/mol

A rigid container initially holds N₂O₄ at 1.00 atm. At equilibrium at 25°C: - P(N₂O₄) = 0.70 atm - P(NO₂) = 0.60 atm

(a) Draw a valid Lewis structure for N₂O₄.

(b) Predict the molecular geometry around each nitrogen atom.

(c) Would this reaction be thermodynamically favored at low or high temperatures? Justify using ΔH° and ΔS°.

(d) Calculate Kₚ for the reaction at 25°C.

(e) If the temperature is increased, will Kₚ increase, decrease, or remain the same? Explain.

Check Yourself: Solution to Practice Question

(a) Lewis Structure of N₂O₄:

    O       O
    ‖       ‖
    N—N
   / \     / \
  O   O   O   O

(Central N-N bond with each N bonded to 2 O atoms)

(b) Molecular Geometry: - Each nitrogen has 3 electron domains (trigonal planar geometry) - Shape around each N: trigonal planar

(c) Temperature Conditions: HIGH TEMPERATURES favored - ΔH° = +58 kJ/mol (positive, endothermic) - ΔS° > 0 (1 mole gas → 2 moles gas, entropy increases) - ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS° - For ΔG° < 0: need large T to make TΔS° > ΔH°

(d) Calculate Kₚ:

Kₚ = [P(NO₂)]² / P(N₂O₄)

Kₚ = (0.60)² / 0.70 = 0.36 / 0.70 = 0.51

(e) Effect of Temperature Increase:

Kₚ will INCREASE

Explanation: The reaction is endothermic (ΔH° > 0). Increasing temperature favors the endothermic direction (forward reaction), shifting equilibrium to produce more NO₂. This increases the equilibrium constant.

General rule: - Endothermic reaction: increasing T increases K - Exothermic reaction: increasing T decreases K

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Forgetting resonance structures in Lewis diagrams
✅ Show one valid structure clearly

❌ Confusing electron geometry with molecular geometry
✅ Lone pairs affect electron geometry but not molecular shape name

❌ Wrong sign analysis for ΔG° predictions
✅ Memorize the 4 cases in the table

❌ Reading graph values carelessly
✅ Use a straight edge to read accurate values

❌ Forgetting to square coefficients in Kₚ expression
✅ Coefficient = exponent in equilibrium expression

Study Tips

  1. Lewis Structures: Practice with molecules that have resonance
  2. VSEPR: Memorize geometries for 2-6 electron domains
  3. Thermodynamics: Know when each ΔH°/ΔS° combination is spontaneous
  4. Equilibrium: Practice reading graphs and calculating K from partial pressures
  5. Le Chatelier: Understand why the system shifts (minimize Q vs K)
