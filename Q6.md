## Question Overview

**Context**: Paraffin wax in building materials melts at 37°C to regulate temperature.
- Molar mass of paraffin: 282.62 g/mol
- Molar heat of fusion: 48.78 kJ/mol

---

## Part (a): Direction of Thermal Energy Flow

### Question
While the wax is melting, is the net flow of thermal energy from the wax to the surroundings or from the surroundings to the wax? Justify your answer.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Identify the type of process
- Melting = solid → liquid
- Is this endothermic or exothermic?

**Step 2**: Apply energy flow principle
- Endothermic: energy flows IN
- Exothermic: energy flows OUT

### Solution

**Answer: From the surroundings TO the wax**

**Justification**:

Melting is an **endothermic process**. Energy must be absorbed by the wax molecules to:
1. Overcome intermolecular forces holding the solid structure together
2. Allow molecules to move more freely in the liquid phase
3. Increase the potential energy of the molecules

Since melting requires energy input, thermal energy must flow **from the surroundings into the wax** for the phase change to occur.

### Key Concepts

**Phase Changes and Energy**:

**Endothermic** (energy absorbed):
- ❄️ → 💧 Melting (solid → liquid)
- 💧 → ☁️ Vaporization (liquid → gas)
- ❄️ → ☁️ Sublimation (solid → gas)

**Exothermic** (energy released):
- 💧 → ❄️ Freezing (liquid → solid)
- ☁️ → 💧 Condensation (gas → liquid)
- ☁️ → ❄️ Deposition (gas → solid)

**Why melting is endothermic**:
- Breaking intermolecular forces requires energy
- Potential energy increases (kinetic energy stays constant during phase change)

## Part (b): Heat of Fusion Calculation

### Question
Calculate the amount of thermal energy, in kJ, required to melt 15.2 grams of solid paraffin wax when the temperature of the surroundings is above the melting point.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Convert grams to moles
moles = mass / molar mass

**Step 2**: Use heat of fusion
q = n × ΔHfus

**Step 3**: Calculate with correct units

### Solution

**Step 1: Calculate moles of paraffin**

n = mass / molar mass

n = 15.2 g / 282.62 g/mol

n = 0.05379 mol

**Step 2: Calculate energy required**

q = n × ΔHfus

q = (0.05379 mol) × (48.78 kJ/mol)

q = 2.624 kJ

**Answer: q = 2.62 kJ** (or 2.6 kJ with appropriate sig figs)

### Key Concepts

**Heat of Fusion (ΔHfus)**:
- Energy required to melt 1 mole of solid at its melting point
- Also called enthalpy of fusion
- Always positive for melting (endothermic)
- Negative for freezing (exothermic)

**Calculation Setup**:
```
q = n × ΔHfus

where:
- q = heat energy (kJ)
- n = moles
- ΔHfus = molar heat of fusion (kJ/mol)
```

**Significant Figures**:
- 15.2 g → 3 sig figs
- 282.62 g/mol → 5 sig figs
- 48.78 kJ/mol → 4 sig figs
- Answer: 3 sig figs (limited by 15.2)

## Part (c): Particle-Level Density Explanation

### Question
When paraffin wax melts, its density decreases. Provide a particle-level explanation of this observation.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Define density
density = mass / volume

**Step 2**: Consider what changes during melting
- Mass stays the same
- What happens to volume?

**Step 3**: Explain using particle arrangement

### Solution

**Particle-Level Explanation**:

**In the solid phase**, paraffin molecules are arranged in a more **ordered, tightly packed structure** with molecules held in relatively fixed positions by intermolecular forces.

**In the liquid phase**, paraffin molecules have **greater freedom of movement** and are **more spread out on average**. The average distance between molecules increases.

**Result**: The same number of molecules (same mass) occupies a **larger volume** when liquid than when solid.

**Since density = mass/volume**, and volume increases while mass stays constant, the **density decreases** upon melting.

**Alternative explanation**:
In liquid paraffin, the average intermolecular distance is greater than in solid paraffin. With the same number of molecules spread over a larger volume, the density (mass per unit volume) decreases.

### Key Concepts

**Density and Phase Changes**:

**Typical behavior** (most substances):
- Solid > Liquid > Gas (density decreases)
- Molecules spread out more as temperature increases

**Exception - Water**:
- Ice (solid) has LOWER density than liquid water
- Ice floats on water
- Due to hydrogen bonding creating open hexagonal structure

**Particle Explanations Must Include**:
1. ✅ Change in spacing/arrangement of particles
2. ✅ Connection to macroscopic property (density, volume)
3. ✅ Correct reasoning (more space = larger volume = lower density)

**What NOT to say**:
- ❌ "Molecules expand" (molecules don't change size)
- ❌ "Molecules get lighter" (mass doesn't change)
- ✅ "Average distance between molecules increases"

## Try It Now! Practice Question

### Modified Question 6

Ice has some unique properties compared to most substances. Consider the following data:

- Molar mass of water: 18.02 g/mol
- Molar heat of fusion of ice: 6.01 kJ/mol
- Density of ice at 0°C: 0.917 g/cm³
- Density of liquid water at 0°C: 1.000 g/cm³

**(a)** When ice melts, does thermal energy flow into the ice or out of the ice? Justify your answer.

**(b)** Calculate the amount of energy, in kJ, required to melt a 50.0 g ice cube.

**(c)** Unlike most substances, ice has a LOWER density than liquid water. Provide a particle-level explanation for why ice is less dense than liquid water. (Hint: Consider hydrogen bonding.)

**(d)** Calculate the volume occupied by 50.0 g of ice at 0°C.

---

## Check Yourself: Solution to Practice Question

**(a) Direction of Energy Flow**:

**Thermal energy flows INTO the ice** (from surroundings to ice)

**Justification**:
Melting is an endothermic process. The ice must absorb energy to:
- Break hydrogen bonds holding the crystalline structure
- Convert from ordered solid to more disordered liquid
- Increase the potential energy of the water molecules

Therefore, energy flows from the warmer surroundings into the ice.

**(b) Energy to Melt Ice**:

**Step 1**: Calculate moles of water

n = 50.0 g / 18.02 g/mol = 2.775 mol

**Step 2**: Calculate energy

q = n × ΔHfus

q = (2.775 mol) × (6.01 kJ/mol)

q = 16.68 kJ

**Answer: q = 16.7 kJ** (3 sig figs)

**(c) Particle-Level Explanation for Lower Density of Ice**:

In **ice**, water molecules form a **crystalline lattice structure** held together by hydrogen bonds. This structure has a specific geometry where each water molecule is hydrogen-bonded to four neighboring molecules in a **tetrahedral arrangement**.

This arrangement creates an **open hexagonal structure with significant empty space** between molecules. The molecules are locked in fixed positions with large gaps in the crystal structure.

In **liquid water**, the hydrogen bond network is partially broken, allowing molecules to move more freely. Molecules can **pack more closely together** and fill some of the empty spaces that existed in the crystalline structure.

**Result**: The same mass of molecules occupies a **smaller volume** in the liquid phase than in the solid phase, making liquid water **denser** than ice.

This is unusual because most substances have higher density in the solid phase.

**(d) Volume of Ice**:

**Using density = mass/volume**:

V = mass / density

V = 50.0 g / 0.917 g/cm³

V = 54.5 cm³

**Answer: 54.5 cm³** (or 0.0545 L)

**Comparison**: 50.0 g of liquid water occupies 50.0 cm³ (since density = 1.000 g/cm³), so ice occupies about 9% more volume than liquid water of the same mass.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Saying "molecules expand" or "molecules get bigger"**  
✅ Molecules themselves don't change; spacing between molecules increases

❌ **Confusing heat of fusion with heat of vaporization**  
✅ Fusion = melting (solid ↔ liquid), Vaporization = boiling (liquid ↔ gas)

❌ **Wrong direction of energy flow**  
✅ Endothermic processes absorb energy (melting, vaporizing, subliming)

❌ **Forgetting to convert grams to moles**  
✅ Always convert mass → moles → energy using molar heat of fusion

❌ **Assuming all solids are denser than their liquids**  
✅ Most are, but water is a notable exception

1. **Memorize phase change types**:
   - Melting/Fusion, Vaporization/Boiling, Sublimation (all endothermic)
   - Freezing, Condensation, Deposition (all exothermic)

2. **Energy calculations**: Always go mass → moles → energy

3. **Particle explanations**: Focus on spacing and arrangement, not particle size

4. **Density relationship**: ρ = m/V
   - Same mass, larger volume → lower density

5. **Water's uniqueness**: Ice floats because hydrogen bonding creates open structure

## Additional Thermochemistry Concepts

**Heating Curves**:
- Temperature stays constant during phase changes
- All energy goes into changing PE (breaking/forming bonds)
- KE (temperature) only changes when not changing phases

**Heat Calculations Summary**:
- **Temperature change**: q = mcΔT (specific heat)
- **Phase change**: q = nΔH (heat of fusion or vaporization)

**Comparison of Phase Changes**:
| Transition | Name | ΔH (relative) | Energy |
|------------|------|---------------|---------|
| S → L | Melting | Small + | Absorb |
| L → G | Vaporization | Large + | Absorb |
| S → G | Sublimation | Very large + | Absorb |
| L → S | Freezing | Small - | Release |
| G → L | Condensation | Large - | Release |
| G → S | Deposition | Very large - | Release |

**Why vaporization > fusion**:
- Vaporization breaks ALL intermolecular forces (complete separation)
- Melting only breaks some forces (partial separation)
- Therefore, ΔHvap >> ΔHfus
