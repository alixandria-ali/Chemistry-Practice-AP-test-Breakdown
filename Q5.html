Question 5: NADH Electrochemistry (4 points)

Units Covered

  • Unit 9: Applications of Thermodynamics (Electrochemistry, Redox reactions, Cell potentials, Gibbs free energy)

Time: ~9 minutes | Points: 4

Question Overview

Half-Reactions: - O₂ + 4 H⁺ + 4 e⁻ → 2 H₂O E° = +0.815 V (reduction) - NAD⁺ + 2 H⁺ + 2 e⁻ → NADH E° = -0.320 V (reduction)

Overall Reaction: O₂ + 2 NADH + 2 H⁺ → 2 H₂O + 2 NAD⁺

Part (a): Moles of Electrons Transferred

Question

How many moles of electrons are transferred for each mole of O₂ that reacts? Justify in terms of the balanced equation and the change in oxidation number of oxygen.

How to Approach

Method 1: Use the half-reaction - Look at the oxygen reduction half-reaction

Method 2: Track oxidation number change - O₂: oxidation state = 0 - H₂O: oxidation state = -2 - Calculate total change

Solution

Answer: 4 moles of electrons per mole of O₂

Justification Method 1 (Half-Reaction):

From the reduction half-reaction: O₂ + 4 H⁺ + 4 e⁻ → 2 H₂O

This shows that 4 electrons are needed to reduce one molecule (or mole) of O₂.

Justification Method 2 (Oxidation Numbers):

O₂ → 2 H₂O - Each O atom: 0 → -2 (change of -2 per atom) - Two O atoms in O₂: 2 × (-2) = -4 total change - A change of -4 means gaining 4 electrons - Therefore, 4 moles of electrons are transferred per mole of O₂

Key Concepts

Oxidation Number Rules: - Element in standard state: 0 - Oxygen in compounds: usually -2 - Change in oxidation number = electrons transferred

Balancing electrons: - Reduction: gain electrons (decrease in oxidation #) - Oxidation: lose electrons (increase in oxidation #)

Part (b): Cell Potential Calculation

Question

Calculate the electrochemical potential (E°cell) of the overall reaction.

How to Approach

Step 1: Identify cathode and anode - Cathode (reduction): higher E° = O₂ reduction (+0.815 V) - Anode (oxidation): lower E° = NADH oxidation (reverse of NAD⁺ reduction)

Step 2: Use the equation E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode

Step 3: Calculate

Solution

Cathode (reduction): O₂ + 4 H⁺ + 4 e⁻ → 2 H₂O E° = +0.815 V

Anode (oxidation): NADH → NAD⁺ + 2 H⁺ + 2 e⁻ E° = -(-0.320 V) = +0.320 V (Note: Oxidation is the reverse of the given reduction half-reaction)

Calculate E°cell:

E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode

E°cell = (+0.815 V) - (-0.320 V)

E°cell = 0.815 V + 0.320 V

E°cell = +1.135 V

Key Concepts

Standard Cell Potential: - E°cell = E°reduction (cathode) - E°reduction (anode) - Alternative: E°cell = E°cathode + E°anode (if anode potential already flipped) - Positive E°cell → spontaneous reaction - More positive E° → stronger oxidizing agent - More negative E° → stronger reducing agent

Remember: - Cathode: reduction occurs (gains electrons) - Anode: oxidation occurs (loses electrons) - Red Cat = Reduction at Cathode

Part (c): Gibbs Free Energy Calculation

Question

Calculate the value of ΔG° for the overall reaction.

How to Approach

Step 1: Use the relationship between ΔG° and E°cell ΔG° = -nFE°cell

Step 2: Determine n (moles of electrons) - From part (a): 4 moles e⁻ per mole O₂

Step 3: Plug in values and calculate

Solution

Equation: ΔG° = -nFE°cell

Where: - n = 4 moles e⁻ (from part a) - F = 96,485 C/mol (Faraday's constant) - E°cell = 1.135 V (from part b)

Substitution:

ΔG° = -(4 mol e⁻) × (96,485 C/mol e⁻) × (1.135 V)

ΔG° = -(4) × (96,485) × (1.135) J/mol

ΔG° = -438,000 J/mol

ΔG° = -438 kJ/mol

(Accept: -438.0 kJ/mol or -4.380 × 10⁵ J/mol)

Key Concepts

Relationship Between ΔG° and E°cell:

ΔG° = -nFE°cell

Sign relationships: - E°cell > 0 → ΔG° < 0 → spontaneous - E°cell < 0 → ΔG° > 0 → nonspontaneous - E°cell = 0 → ΔG° = 0 → at equilibrium

Units: - 1 V = 1 J/C (volt = joule/coulomb) - F = 96,485 C/mol e⁻ - Result in Joules, convert to kJ

Remember: The negative sign means a positive cell potential gives a negative (favorable) ΔG°

Part (d): Effect of Concentration Changes

Question

When [NADH] increases and [NAD⁺] decreases, will the electrochemical potential increase, decrease, or remain the same? Justify your answer.

How to Approach

Step 1: Use the Nernst equation concept E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q

Step 2: Determine how Q changes Q = [products]/[reactants]

Step 3: Analyze the effect

Solution

Answer: The electrochemical potential will INCREASE

Justification Method 1 (Nernst Equation):

The Nernst equation: E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q

Where Q = [NAD⁺]²/[NADH]²[H⁺]²[O₂]

When [NADH] increases and [NAD⁺] decreases: - Numerator of Q decreases ([NAD⁺] decreases) - Denominator of Q increases ([NADH] increases) - Therefore, Q decreases - Since ln Q becomes more negative, the term -(RT/nF) ln Q becomes more positive - Result: E increases (becomes more positive)

Justification Method 2 (Le Chatelier/ΔG):

The overall reaction: O₂ + 2 NADH + 2 H⁺ → 2 H₂O + 2 NAD⁺

  • Increasing [NADH] and decreasing [NAD⁺] means increasing reactants and decreasing products
  • This makes Q smaller (Q moves away from K)
  • The system is further from equilibrium
  • Larger driving force → more negative ΔG → more positive E

Justification Method 3 (Intuition):

ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q

  • Decreasing Q makes RT ln Q more negative
  • This makes ΔG more negative (more spontaneous)
  • Since ΔG = -nFE, more negative ΔG means more positive E

Key Concepts

Nernst Equation:

E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q

Or at 25°C: E = E° - (0.0592 V/n) log Q

Reaction Quotient (Q): - Q = [products]/[reactants] - When Q < K: reaction proceeds forward, E > E° - When Q > K: reaction proceeds backward, E < E° - When Q = K: at equilibrium, E = 0

Effect of Concentrations: - Increasing reactants → Q decreases → E increases - Increasing products → Q increases → E decreases

Complete Answer Summary

(b) 1 E°cell = +1.135 V

Modified Question 5

Consider the electrochemical cell based on these half-reactions:

  • Cu²⁺ + 2 e⁻ → Cu(s) E° = +0.34 V
  • Zn²⁺ + 2 e⁻ → Zn(s) E° = -0.76 V

The overall reaction is: Cu²⁺ + Zn(s) → Cu(s) + Zn²⁺

(a) How many moles of electrons are transferred per mole of Cu²⁺ reduced?

(b) Calculate E°cell for this reaction.

(c) Calculate ΔG° for this reaction.

(d) If the concentration of Cu²⁺ is increased while [Zn²⁺] stays constant, will the cell potential increase, decrease, or remain the same? Explain.

(e) Calculate the cell potential at 25°C when [Cu²⁺] = 0.010 M and [Zn²⁺] = 1.00 M.

Check Yourself: Solution to Practice Question

(a) Electrons Transferred:

2 moles of electrons per mole of Cu²⁺

From half-reaction: Cu²⁺ + 2 e⁻ → Cu - Cu²⁺ (oxidation state +2) → Cu (oxidation state 0) - Change: +2 → 0 (decrease of 2) - Must gain 2 electrons

(b) Calculate E°cell:

Cathode (reduction): Cu²⁺ + 2 e⁻ → Cu E° = +0.34 V

Anode (oxidation): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2 e⁻ E° = -0.76 V

E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode

E°cell = (+0.34 V) - (-0.76 V) = 0.34 + 0.76

E°cell = +1.10 V

(c) Calculate ΔG°:

ΔG° = -nFE°cell

ΔG° = -(2 mol e⁻)(96,485 C/mol)(1.10 V)

ΔG° = -212,267 J/mol

ΔG° = -212 kJ/mol

(d) Effect of Increasing [Cu²⁺]:

The cell potential will INCREASE

Explanation: - Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] (products over reactants) - Increasing [Cu²⁺] (denominator) decreases Q - By Nernst equation: E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q - Smaller Q → smaller ln Q → less subtraction from E° - Result: E increases

(e) Calculate Cell Potential with Concentrations:

Nernst Equation at 25°C:

E = E° - (0.0592/n) log Q

Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] = 1.00/0.010 = 100

E = 1.10 V - (0.0592/2) log(100)

E = 1.10 V - (0.0296) × (2)

E = 1.10 V - 0.059 V

E = 1.04 V

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Forgetting to flip the sign when reversing a half-reaction
✅ If reversing from reduction to oxidation, flip the E° sign

❌ Multiplying E° when multiplying half-reactions
✅ E° is intensive; don't multiply it even if you multiply the equation

❌ Wrong number of electrons in ΔG calculation
✅ Use the total moles transferred in the balanced overall equation

❌ Forgetting the negative sign in ΔG° = -nFE°
✅ Always include the negative sign

❌ Confusing Q and K in Nernst equation
✅ Use Q (actual concentrations), not K (equilibrium constant)

Study Tips

  1. Memorize key equations:
  2. E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode
  3. ΔG° = -nFE°

  4. E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q

  5. Sign conventions:

  6. Positive E° → spontaneous
  7. Negative ΔG° → spontaneous

  8. These always agree!

  9. Practice electron counting: Track oxidation number changes carefully

  10. Nernst equation shortcuts: At 25°C, use 0.0592/n for quick calculations

  11. Q vs K: Q uses actual concentrations, K uses equilibrium concentrations

Additional Electrochemistry Concepts

Standard Reduction Potentials: - Measured vs Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE): 2 H⁺ + 2 e⁻ → H₂, E° = 0.00 V - More positive E° = stronger oxidizing agent (wants to be reduced) - More negative E° = stronger reducing agent (wants to be oxidized)

Electrolytic vs Galvanic Cells: - Galvanic: Spontaneous (E° > 0), generates electricity - Electrolytic: Nonspontaneous (E° < 0), requires electricity input

Relationship to Equilibrium:

E° = (RT/nF) ln K or at 25°C: E° = (0.0592/n) log K

  • Large positive E° → large K → products strongly favored
  • Large negative E° → small K → reactants strongly favored
