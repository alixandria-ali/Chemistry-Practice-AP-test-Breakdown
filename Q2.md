# Question 2: Ionic Bonding

## Unit: Unit 2 - Molecular and Ionic Compound Structure and Properties
**Topic**: Ionic Bonding (SAP-2.A)

---

## Original Question
Which pair of elements can form ionic bonds?
- A. copper (Cu) and nitrogen (N)
- B. gallium (Ga) and phosphorus (P)
- C. hydrogen (H) and sulfur (S)
- D. phosphorus (P) and fluorine (F)
- E. potassium (K) and bromine (Br)

---

## How to Approach This Question

### Step 1: Understand Ionic Bonding
Ionic bonding occurs when there is a **transfer of electrons** from one atom to another, creating:
- **Cations** (positive ions) - formed by metals losing electrons
- **Anions** (negative ions) - formed by nonmetals gaining electrons

### Step 2: Identify Metals and Nonmetals Using the Periodic Table
**Metals** (left and middle):
- Groups 1-2 (alkali and alkaline earth metals)
- Transition metals (Groups 3-12)
- Some post-transition metals (Al, Ga, In, Sn, Pb)

**Nonmetals** (right side):
- Groups 14-18 (especially 15-17)
- Hydrogen (unique case)

**Metalloids** (staircase boundary): Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te - show intermediate properties

### Step 3: Evaluate Each Pair

**A. Cu (copper) and N (nitrogen)**
- Cu = transition metal (metal)
- N = nonmetal
- ✓ Metal + Nonmetal = Can form ionic compound
- Cu₃N or Cu₃N₂ (copper nitride)

**B. Ga (gallium) and P (phosphorus)**
- Ga = post-transition metal
- P = nonmetal
- ✓ Metal + Nonmetal = Can form ionic compound
- GaP (gallium phosphide) - though this has significant covalent character

**C. H (hydrogen) and S (sulfur)**
- Both are nonmetals
- ✗ Nonmetal + Nonmetal = Covalent bonding
- H₂S (hydrogen sulfide) - covalent

**D. P (phosphorus) and F (fluorine)**
- Both are nonmetals
- ✗ Nonmetal + Nonmetal = Covalent bonding
- PF₃, PF₅ - covalent

**E. K (potassium) and Br (bromine)**
- K = Group 1 alkali metal (highly reactive metal)
- Br = Group 17 halogen (highly reactive nonmetal)
- ✓ Metal + Nonmetal = Strong ionic compound
- KBr (potassium bromide)

### Step 4: Choose the MOST Clearly Ionic
While A, B, and E can all form ionic bonds, **E (K and Br)** forms the **most clearly ionic** compound because:
- K is in Group 1 (loses 1 electron easily)
- Br is in Group 17 (gains 1 electron easily)
- Large electronegativity difference
- Forms 1:1 ratio ionic compound

**Answer: E**

---

## Key Concepts to Remember

1. **Ionic bonding**: Metal + Nonmetal (electron transfer)
2. **Covalent bonding**: Nonmetal + Nonmetal (electron sharing)
3. **Metallic bonding**: Metal + Metal (electron sea)
4. **Electronegativity difference**:
   - ΔEN > 1.7 → typically ionic
   - ΔEN < 1.7 → typically covalent
   - (This is a guideline, not absolute)

## Try It Now!

**Practice Question:**

Which of the following compounds is most likely to exhibit ionic bonding?

A. SiO₂ (silicon dioxide)
B. CaO (calcium oxide)
C. CO₂ (carbon dioxide)
D. NO₂ (nitrogen dioxide)
E. PCl₃ (phosphorus trichloride)

---

## Check Yourself

### Your Answer: ____

### Feedback

**Approach:**
1. Identify the types of elements in each compound
2. Look for **metal + nonmetal** combinations

**Analysis:**
- **A. SiO₂**: Si is a metalloid, O is a nonmetal → covalent (network covalent)
- **B. CaO**: Ca is a Group 2 metal, O is a nonmetal → **IONIC**
- **C. CO₂**: Both C and O are nonmetals → covalent
- **D. NO₂**: Both N and O are nonmetals → covalent
- **E. PCl₃**: Both P and Cl are nonmetals → covalent

**Correct Answer: B**

CaO (calcium oxide) is an ionic compound. Calcium (Group 2) readily loses 2 electrons to form Ca²⁺, and oxygen readily gains 2 electrons to form O²⁻. The resulting electrostatic attraction creates a strong ionic bond. CaO is also known as quicklime and has typical ionic properties: high melting point, conducts electricity when molten, and forms a crystal lattice structure.
