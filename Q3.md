# Question 3: Intermolecular Forces and Boiling Points

## Unit: Unit 2 - Molecular and Ionic Compound Structure and Properties
**Topic**: Intermolecular Forces (SAP-5.A, SAP-5.B)

---

## Original Question
Consider the substances hydrogen (H₂), fluorine (F₂), and hydrogen fluoride (HF). Based on their molecular structures, how does the boiling point of HF compare with the boiling points of H₂ and F₂?

The boiling point of HF is:
- [higher than / lower than / similar to] the boiling point of H₂, and it is
- [higher than / lower than / similar to] the boiling point of F₂.

---

## How to Approach This Question

### Step 1: Identify the Intermolecular Forces (IMFs) in Each Substance

**H₂ (hydrogen gas)**:
- Nonpolar molecule (H-H bond has no polarity)
- **Only London dispersion forces** (weakest IMF)
- Very small molecular size → very weak dispersion forces

**F₂ (fluorine gas)**:
- Nonpolar molecule (F-F bond between identical atoms)
- **Only London dispersion forces**
- Slightly larger than H₂ → slightly stronger dispersion forces than H₂

**HF (hydrogen fluoride)**:
- **Highly polar molecule** (large electronegativity difference)
- H is bonded directly to F (highly electronegative)
- Can form **hydrogen bonds** (strongest IMF for molecules)

### Step 2: Rank the Intermolecular Forces by Strength

**Strength of IMFs:**
Hydrogen bonding > Dipole-dipole > London dispersion forces

**For these three substances:**
1. **HF**: Hydrogen bonding (strongest)
2. **F₂**: London dispersion (stronger than H₂ due to more electrons)
3. **H₂**: London dispersion (weakest)

### Step 3: Connect IMF Strength to Boiling Point

**Stronger IMF → Higher boiling point**

Why? More energy is needed to overcome stronger intermolecular attractions to convert liquid to gas.

### Step 4: Make the Comparisons

- **HF vs. H₂**: HF has hydrogen bonding, H₂ has only weak dispersion → **HF has HIGHER boiling point**
- **HF vs. F₂**: HF has hydrogen bonding, F₂ has only dispersion → **HF has HIGHER boiling point**

**Answer: The boiling point of HF is HIGHER THAN the boiling point of H₂, and it is HIGHER THAN the boiling point of F₂.**

---

## Actual Boiling Points (for reference)
- H₂: -253°C
- F₂: -188°C
- HF: +20°C

Notice HF is actually a **liquid at room temperature**, while H₂ and F₂ are gases!

---

## Key Concepts to Remember

### Types of Intermolecular Forces (weakest to strongest):

1. **London Dispersion Forces**
   - Present in ALL molecules
   - Only IMF in nonpolar molecules
   - Strength increases with molecular size/electrons

2. **Dipole-Dipole Forces**
   - Present in polar molecules
   - Permanent partial charges attract

3. **Hydrogen Bonding**
   - Special type of dipole-dipole
   - Requires H bonded to N, O, or F
   - Strongest IMF

### Properties Affected by IMFs:
- Boiling point
- Melting point
- Vapor pressure (inverse relationship)
- Viscosity
- Surface tension

### Feedback

**Approach:**
1. Identify the IMFs present in each molecule
2. Rank the IMF strengths
3. Arrange in order of increasing boiling point

**Analysis:**

**CH₄ (methane)**:
- Nonpolar molecule (tetrahedral, symmetrical)
- Only London dispersion forces
- Small molecule → very weak dispersion

**NH₃ (ammonia)**:
- Polar molecule
- H bonded to N → can form **hydrogen bonds**
- 3 H atoms available for H-bonding

**H₂O (water)**:
- Polar molecule
- H bonded to O → can form **hydrogen bonds**
- O is more electronegative than N → **stronger H-bonds**
- Can form more H-bonds per molecule (2 H atoms, 2 lone pairs on O)

**Ranking:**
1. CH₄: Only weak dispersion forces
2. NH₃: Hydrogen bonding (moderate strength)
3. H₂O: Hydrogen bonding (strongest)

**Correct Answer: A (CH₄ < NH₃ < H₂O)**

Water has the highest boiling point (100°C) due to extensive hydrogen bonding. Ammonia has a moderate boiling point (-33°C) with hydrogen bonding but fewer H-bonds than water. Methane has the lowest boiling point (-162°C) with only weak London dispersion forces.
