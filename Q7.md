## Part (a): Hydrogen Ion Concentration

### Question
The pH of the solution is 11.20. Calculate the hydrogen ion concentration, [H⁺], in the solution.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Recall the pH definition
pH = -log[H⁺]

**Step 2**: Rearrange to solve for [H⁺]
[H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ

**Step 3**: Calculate

### Solution

**Equation**: pH = -log[H⁺]

**Rearranged**: [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ

**Substitute pH = 11.20**:

[H⁺] = 10⁻¹¹·²⁰

[H⁺] = 6.31 × 10⁻¹² M

**Answer: [H⁺] = 6.3 × 10⁻¹² M**

(Accept: 6.31 × 10⁻¹² M with appropriate sig figs)

### Key Concepts

**pH and [H⁺] Relationship**:

**pH = -log[H⁺]**   or   **[H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ**

**pH Scale**:
- pH < 7: acidic ([H⁺] > 1 × 10⁻⁷ M)
- pH = 7: neutral ([H⁺] = 1 × 10⁻⁷ M)
- pH > 7: basic ([H⁺] < 1 × 10⁻⁷ M)

**Calculator Tips**:
- For [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ: Use "10^x" or "inv log" function
- Input: -11.20
- Result: 6.31 × 10⁻¹²

**Quick Check**:
- pH 11.20 is basic ✓
- [H⁺] is very small (< 10⁻⁷) ✓
- Makes sense!

## Part (b): Concentration from Calibration Curve

### Question
Based on the calibration curve, what is the concentration of the solution in micromoles per liter (μM)?

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Locate the absorbance on the y-axis (0.35)

**Step 2**: Draw a horizontal line to the calibration curve

**Step 3**: Draw a vertical line down to the x-axis

**Step 4**: Read the concentration value

### Solution

**From the calibration curve**:

Absorbance = 0.35 corresponds to:

**Concentration = 5.4 μM**

(Acceptable range: 5.2-5.6 μM depending on graph reading accuracy)

### Key Concepts

**Calibration Curves**:
- Plot of absorbance vs. concentration for known standards
- Used to determine unknown concentrations
- Linear relationship (Beer's Law)
- Must interpolate (read within the range), not extrapolate (beyond range)

**Beer's Law**: A = εbc
- A = absorbance (no units)
- ε = molar absorptivity (M⁻¹cm⁻¹)
- b = path length (cm)
- c = concentration (M)

**Using Calibration Curves**:
1. Find measured absorbance on y-axis
2. Trace horizontally to curve
3. Trace vertically to x-axis
4. Read concentration

## Part (c): Effect of Dilution on Absorbance

### Question
If the student mixed 10 mL of distilled water with 10 mL of the sample, would the diluted solution have an absorbance greater than, less than, or equal to the absorbance of the original solution? Justify your answer.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Determine the dilution factor
- Original: 10 mL sample
- Diluted: 10 mL sample + 10 mL water = 20 mL total

**Step 2**: Apply Beer's Law

**Step 3**: Relate concentration to absorbance

### Solution

**Answer: The absorbance would be LESS THAN the original absorbance**

**Justification**:

**Dilution Calculation**:
- Original volume: 10 mL
- Final volume: 20 mL
- Dilution factor: 20/10 = 2
- New concentration = Original concentration / 2

**Beer's Law Application**:

A = εbc

- ε (molar absorptivity) is constant for phenol red at 559 nm
- b (path length) is constant (same cuvette)
- c (concentration) is cut in half

Since **absorbance is directly proportional to concentration**, cutting the concentration in half will **cut the absorbance in half**.

**Specific Example**:
- Original: A = 0.35, c = 5.4 μM
- Diluted: A = 0.175, c = 2.7 μM

### Key Concepts

**Beer's Law Proportionality**:

**A = εbc**

When ε and b are constant:
- **A ∝ c** (absorbance is directly proportional to concentration)
- Double concentration → double absorbance
- Halve concentration → halve absorbance

**Dilution Formula**:

**M₁V₁ = M₂V₂**   or   **C₁V₁ = C₂V₂**

Example: 10 mL of sample + 10 mL water
- V₁ = 10 mL, V₂ = 20 mL
- C₂ = C₁ × (V₁/V₂) = C₁ × (10/20) = C₁/2

**Dilution Factor**:
- V₂/V₁ = 20/10 = 2 (diluted by factor of 2)
- Concentration decreases by factor of 2
- Absorbance decreases by factor of 2

## Part (d): Absorbance at Different Wavelength

### Question
If the student measured the absorbance of the solution at 650 nm, would the absorbance be greater than, less than, or equal to the absorbance at 559 nm? Justify your answer.

### How to Approach

**Step 1**: Identify the maximum absorbance wavelength (λmax)
- Given: λmax = 559 nm

**Step 2**: Understand what λmax means
- Wavelength where substance absorbs most strongly

**Step 3**: Compare 650 nm to 559 nm

### Solution

**Answer: The absorbance at 650 nm would be LESS THAN the absorbance at 559 nm**

**Justification**:

559 nm is stated as the **wavelength of maximum absorbance** (λmax) for phenol red. This means phenol red absorbs light most strongly at this wavelength.

At any other wavelength, including 650 nm, phenol red absorbs **less light** than at 559 nm. The molar absorptivity (ε) at 650 nm is lower than the molar absorptivity at 559 nm.

**Using Beer's Law**: A = εbc
- b and c are the same regardless of wavelength
- ε is smaller at 650 nm than at 559 nm
- Therefore, A is smaller at 650 nm

**Analogy**: It's like trying to hear a specific note - you hear it loudest at its natural frequency (559 nm for phenol red) and quieter at other frequencies (650 nm).

### Key Concepts

**Wavelength and Absorbance**:

**λmax (Lambda max)**:
- Wavelength of maximum absorbance
- Where the substance absorbs most strongly
- Where ε (molar absorptivity) is highest
- Best wavelength for measurements (most sensitive)

**Absorption Spectrum**:
- Plot of absorbance vs. wavelength
- Shows peak at λmax
- Lower absorbance at wavelengths away from λmax

**Why λmax Matters**:
- Most accurate measurements at λmax
- Highest sensitivity to concentration changes
- Minimizes error in concentration determination

**Beer's Law at Different Wavelengths**:
- At λmax: A = εmax × b × c (largest A)
- At other λ: A = ε × b × c (smaller A)
- Same concentration, different absorbance based on ε

## Try It Now! Practice Question

### Modified Question 7

A student analyzes a solution of bromothymol blue (an acid-base indicator):
- pH of solution: 9.50
- Absorbance at 620 nm (λmax): 0.48
- Molar absorptivity at 620 nm: 8.0 × 10⁴ M⁻¹cm⁻¹
- Path length: 1.0 cm

**(a)** Calculate [H⁺] in the solution.

**(b)** Calculate pOH of the solution.

**(c)** Use Beer's Law to calculate the concentration of bromothymol blue in mol/L.

**(d)** The student dilutes 5.0 mL of this solution to a final volume of 25.0 mL. What will be the absorbance of the diluted solution at 620 nm?

**(e)** If the student measured absorbance at 450 nm instead of 620 nm, would you expect the absorbance to be higher or lower? Explain.

---

## Check Yourself: Solution to Practice Question

**(a) Calculate [H⁺]**:

[H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ

[H⁺] = 10⁻⁹·⁵⁰

**[H⁺] = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁰ M**

**(b) Calculate pOH**:

At 25°C: pH + pOH = 14

pOH = 14 - pH

pOH = 14 - 9.50

**pOH = 4.50**

**(c) Calculate Concentration Using Beer's Law**:

**Beer's Law**: A = εbc

Rearrange: c = A / (εb)

c = 0.48 / [(8.0 × 10⁴ M⁻¹cm⁻¹)(1.0 cm)]

c = 0.48 / (8.0 × 10⁴ M⁻¹)

c = 6.0 × 10⁻⁶ M

**c = 6.0 × 10⁻⁶ M** or **6.0 μM**

**(d) Absorbance After Dilution**:

**Step 1**: Calculate dilution factor

V₁ = 5.0 mL, V₂ = 25.0 mL

Dilution factor = V₂/V₁ = 25.0/5.0 = 5

**Step 2**: Calculate new concentration

C₂ = C₁/5 = (6.0 × 10⁻⁶ M)/5 = 1.2 × 10⁻⁶ M

**Step 3**: Calculate new absorbance

Since A ∝ c, and concentration decreased by factor of 5:

A₂ = A₁/5 = 0.48/5 = 0.096

**A₂ = 0.096** (or 0.10 with 2 sig figs)

**Alternative method using M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ and A ∝ c**:

Since absorbance is proportional to concentration, and concentration is proportional to the dilution:

A₂ = A₁ × (V₁/V₂) = 0.48 × (5.0/25.0) = 0.48 × 0.20 = 0.096

**(e) Absorbance at Different Wavelength**:

**The absorbance at 450 nm would be LOWER**

**Explanation**:
620 nm is the wavelength of maximum absorbance (λmax) for bromothymol blue. At this wavelength, the molar absorptivity is at its highest value. At any other wavelength, including 450 nm, the molar absorptivity will be lower, resulting in lower absorbance for the same concentration and path length.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Confusing pH and [H⁺]** - they're inversely related  
✅ Higher pH = lower [H⁺]

❌ **Reading calibration curves incorrectly**  
✅ Always: absorbance → curve → concentration

❌ **Thinking dilution increases absorbance**  
✅ Dilution ALWAYS decreases concentration and absorbance

❌ **Assuming all wavelengths give same absorbance**  
✅ Absorbance is highest at λmax, lower at other wavelengths

❌ **Forgetting Beer's Law units**  
✅ Make sure units cancel properly: M⁻¹cm⁻¹ × cm × M = dimensionless

## Additional Spectroscopy Concepts

**Why Substances Have Color**:
- Substance absorbs certain wavelengths
- Complementary color is transmitted/reflected
- Phenol red absorbs green (559 nm), appears pink/red

**Complementary Colors**:
| Absorbed | Observed Color |
|----------|----------------|
| Red (~700 nm) | Green |
| Green (~550 nm) | Red/Pink |
| Blue (~450 nm) | Orange |

**Beer's Law Limitations**:
- Only valid at low/moderate concentrations
- Assumes monochromatic light
- Assumes no chemical reactions or aggregation

**Transmittance vs Absorbance**:
- **Transmittance (T)**: fraction of light that passes through
- **A = -log(T)** or **T = 10⁻ᴬ**
- T = 1 (100%) → A = 0 (no absorption)
- T = 0.1 (10%) → A = 1
- T = 0.01 (1%) → A = 2

**Spectrophotometer Components**:
1. Light source
2. Monochromator (selects wavelength)
3. Sample holder (cuvette)
4. Detector
5. Display

**Best Practices**:
- Blank with solvent first
- Use matched cuvettes
- Measure at λmax for best results
- Keep concentration in linear range
- Avoid air bubbles and fingerprints on cuvettes
