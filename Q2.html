Solution

Key Concepts

Particulate Representations: - Each molecule must show the correct number and type of atoms - Maintain conservation of atoms (what goes in must come out) - Use stoichiometric ratios from balanced equation - Black circles = Hydrogen (H) - White circles = Oxygen (O)

Law of Conservation of Mass: - Atoms cannot be created or destroyed - Count atoms on both sides to verify

Part (b): Energy Diagram Analysis

Question

Shown below is a potential energy diagram for the uncatalyzed decomposition of H₂O₂(aq).

According to the diagram, is the decomposition reaction exothermic or endothermic? Justify your answer.

How to Approach

Step 1: Compare energy levels - Look at the energy of reactants (left side) - Look at the energy of products (right side)

Step 2: Determine the relationship - If products are LOWER than reactants → exothermic (releases energy) - If products are HIGHER than reactants → endothermic (absorbs energy)

Step 3: Justify with graph observation

Solution

The reaction is EXOTHERMIC because according to the graph, the reaction is giving off heat.

Also, we know that the reaction is thermodynamically favorable, and when ΔG is negative, the reaction must be exothermic.

Better explanation: The reaction is exothermic because the potential energy of the products (2 H₂O + O₂) is lower than the potential energy of the reactants (2 H₂O₂). The difference in energy is released as heat.

Key Concepts

Energy Diagrams: - Exothermic: Products lower than reactants, ΔH < 0, releases heat - Endothermic: Products higher than reactants, ΔH > 0, absorbs heat

Activation Energy (Ea): - The "hill" or barrier that must be overcome - Height from reactants to the peak - Does NOT determine if reaction is exo/endo

Relationship to ΔH: - ΔH = (Energy of products) - (Energy of reactants) - Negative ΔH = exothermic - Positive ΔH = endothermic

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

For a reaction, the reactants have a potential energy of 150 kJ and the products have a potential energy of 100 kJ. Is this reaction exothermic or endothermic? Calculate ΔH.

Check Yourself

Solution:

Analysis: - Reactants: 150 kJ - Products: 100 kJ - Products are LOWER than reactants

Conclusion: EXOTHERMIC

Calculate ΔH: ΔH = Energy(products) - Energy(reactants) ΔH = 100 - 150 = -50 kJ

Negative ΔH confirms the reaction is exothermic and releases 50 kJ of energy.

Part (c): Catalyst Effect on Energy Diagram

Question

Manganese dioxide, MnO₂(s), is an insoluble substance that acts as a catalyst for the decomposition reaction. On the diagram above, draw a curve to represent the reaction as it occurs in the presence of MnO₂(s).

How to Approach

Step 1: Understand what a catalyst does - Lowers activation energy (Ea) - Provides an alternative pathway - Does NOT change ΔH (reactants and products stay at same energy levels)

Step 2: Draw the new curve - Start at same reactant energy level - Peak should be LOWER than original peak - End at same product energy level - Curve should have similar shape but lower maximum

Solution

The curve I drew has a smaller bump, representing the smaller activation energy needed.

The new curve should: - Begin at the same starting point (2 H₂O₂) - Have a LOWER peak (smaller activation energy) - End at the same endpoint (2 H₂O + O₂) - Show that less energy is needed to start the reaction

Key Concepts

Catalysts: - Speed up reactions by lowering activation energy - Provide an alternative reaction pathway - Are NOT consumed in the reaction - Do NOT change ΔH, equilibrium position, or thermodynamic favorability - Do NOT appear in overall balanced equation

What Catalysts Change: - ✓ Activation energy (Ea) → decreases - ✓ Reaction rate → increases - ✓ Time to reach equilibrium → decreases

What Catalysts DON'T Change: - ✗ ΔH (enthalpy change) - ✗ ΔG (Gibbs free energy) - ✗ Equilibrium constant (K) - ✗ Position of equilibrium - ✗ Yield of products

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

For the decomposition of H₂O₂, the uncatalyzed reaction has Ea = 75 kJ/mol and ΔH = -98 kJ/mol. With MnO₂ catalyst, Ea = 58 kJ/mol. What is ΔH for the catalyzed reaction?

Check Yourself

Solution:

Answer: ΔH = -98 kJ/mol (same as uncatalyzed)

Explanation: Catalysts lower activation energy but do NOT change ΔH. The enthalpy change depends only on the energy difference between reactants and products, which remains constant regardless of the pathway taken.

Question

(i) The number of moles of O₂(g) that was produced

How to Approach

Step 1: Find the mass loss - Initial mass (t=0): 43.19 g - Final mass (t=60): 42.58 g - Mass lost: 43.19 - 42.58 = 0.61 g

Step 2: Identify what was lost - The mass loss is due to O₂(g) escaping - Oxygen gas has molar mass = 32.00 g/mol

Step 3: Convert mass to moles moles = mass / molar mass

Solution

Mass of O₂ lost: 43.19 - 42.58 = 0.61 g

Moles of O₂: n = 0.61 g / 32.00 g/mol = 0.019 mol

Or: 0.019 mol O₂ (can also write as 1.9 × 10⁻² mol)

Key Concepts

Gas Evolution and Mass Loss: - When a gas escapes, the container loses mass - Mass lost = mass of gas that escaped - Use molar mass to convert to moles

Common Molar Masses: - O₂ = 32.00 g/mol - H₂ = 2.016 g/mol - CO₂ = 44.01 g/mol - N₂ = 28.01 g/mol

Part (e): Zero-Order Kinetics Claim

Question

Based on the data, the student claims that the catalyzed reaction has zeroth-order kinetics. Do you agree with the student's claim? Justify your answer.

How to Approach

Step 1: Understand zero-order kinetics - Rate is constant (doesn't change with concentration) - Graphically: [Reactant] vs. time is a straight line with negative slope - Or: mass vs. time shows constant rate of change

Step 2: Analyze the data table Look at the mass change per 10-second interval: - 0-10 s: 43.19 - 43.06 = 0.13 g lost - 10-20 s: 43.06 - 42.94 = 0.12 g lost
- 20-30 s: 42.94 - 42.83 = 0.11 g lost - 30-40 s: 42.83 - 42.73 = 0.10 g lost - 40-50 s: 42.73 - 42.65 = 0.08 g lost - 50-60 s: 42.65 - 42.58 = 0.07 g lost

Step 3: Determine if rate is constant The mass lost per interval is getting smaller → rate is decreasing

Solution

Yes, I agree with this claim. Zero-order kinetics means the sample graphing of the experimental data, with no logs or reciprocals, will be a straight line, and not a logarithmic curve.

Better answer: Yes, I agree. The data shows that every 10 seconds, the difference between values is getting smaller. If this trend continued, graphing the data would yield a straight line (upon graphing I also got this straight line), and not a logarithmic curve, indicating zero-order kinetics.

Actually, analyzing more carefully: The decreasing mass loss per interval suggests the reaction is slowing down, which is NOT characteristic of zero-order kinetics.

Most accurate answer: No, I do not fully agree. While the initial data might appear linear, zero-order kinetics requires a CONSTANT rate. The mass lost per 10-second interval decreases from 0.13 g to 0.07 g, showing the reaction rate is decreasing. This is more consistent with first-order kinetics, where rate decreases as reactant concentration decreases.

However, if the student plotted the data and obtained a straight line, they may be correct for the time period measured. Zero-order kinetics can occur when catalyst sites are saturated.

Key Concepts

Reaction Orders:

Zero-Order: - Rate = k (constant) - [A] vs time = straight line (negative slope) - Half-life increases with time - Often occurs with saturated catalyst or surface reactions

First-Order: - Rate = k[A] - ln[A] vs time = straight line - Constant half-life - Most common for single reactant decomposition

Second-Order: - Rate = k[A]²
- 1/[A] vs time = straight line - Half-life decreases with time

Determining Order: - Plot data different ways - See which gives straight line - Or calculate: if rate constant when [A] changes → zero order

Part (f): Effect of Lab Error

Question

A second student did the experiment using larger volumes of 9.77 M H₂O₂(aq). The student noticed that the reaction in beaker 2 proceeded extremely rapidly, causing some of the liquid to splash out of the beaker onto the lab table. The student claims that as a result of the loss of the liquid from the beaker, the calculated number of moles of O₂(g) produced is greater than the actual number of moles of O₂(g) produced during the first 60 seconds. Do you agree with the student? Justify your answer.

How to Approach

Step 1: Understand what happened - Liquid H₂O₂ splashed out during reaction - This liquid had mass - Mass was lost but NOT due to O₂ production

Step 2: Consider the calculation method - Moles of O₂ calculated from: mass lost / molar mass - Assumption: all mass lost = O₂ gas

Step 3: Analyze the error - Actual mass lost = O₂ gas + liquid splash - Calculated moles O₂ based on total mass lost - This gives MORE moles than actually produced

Solution

Yes, this represents a difference in predicted vs actual and resulted from a lab mistake. This would change the mass recorded in the beaker and therefore would change molarity and experimental data.

Better answer: Yes, I agree. The calculated moles of O₂ would be greater than actual because: - The student calculated moles O₂ from total mass lost from beaker - But the mass lost included BOTH O₂ gas AND H₂O₂ liquid that splashed out - Since more mass was lost than just from O₂, the calculated moles O₂ would be artificially high - The actual moles of O₂ produced would be less than calculated

Key Concepts

Experimental Errors:

Systematic Error (consistent, predictable): - Calibration errors - Faulty equipment - Consistent technique flaws

Random Error (unpredictable): - Reading fluctuations - Environmental variations

This error type: Systematic - consistently overestimates O₂ because liquid loss adds to apparent gas production

Error Analysis: - Always consider: What was measured? - What assumptions were made? - How could the assumption be violated?
