Question Overview

Reaction: C₂H₅OH(l) + 3 O₂(g) → 2 CO₂(g) + 3 H₂O(g)
ΔH°comb = -1270 kJ/molrxn

Part (a): Oxidation-Reduction Analysis

Question

Is O₂(g) oxidized in the reaction, or is it reduced? Justify your answer in terms of oxidation numbers.

How to Approach

Step 1: Assign oxidation numbers to oxygen in reactants and products - O₂(g): Each O has oxidation number = 0 (element in standard state) - CO₂(g): O has oxidation number = -2 - H₂O(g): O has oxidation number = -2

Step 2: Determine the change - Oxygen goes from 0 → -2 - This means oxygen gains electrons

Step 3: Apply the definition - Reduction = gain of electrons - Oxidation = loss of electrons

Solution

O₂ is REDUCED because it is gaining electrons. When it gains electrons, it is reduced. It starts at O with an oxidation number of 0 and then gains electrons to fill its valence shell, ending at an oxidation number of -2.

Key Concepts

OIL RIG Memory Aid: - Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons) - Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

Oxidation Number Rules: 1. Elements in standard state = 0 2. Oxygen in compounds = -2 (usually) 3. Hydrogen = +1 (usually) 4. Sum of oxidation numbers = charge of species

Practice Question:

In the reaction: 2 H₂S(g) + 3 O₂(g) → 2 SO₂(g) + 2 H₂O(g)

Is sulfur oxidized or reduced? Justify using oxidation numbers.

Check Yourself

Approach:

  1. Assign oxidation numbers to sulfur in reactants and products
  2. Determine the change
  3. Identify as oxidation or reduction

Solution:

Step 1: Oxidation numbers - H₂S: S = -2 (H is +1, so S must be -2) - SO₂: S = +4 (O is -2 each, so S must be +4)

Step 2: Change - Sulfur goes from -2 → +4 - This means sulfur loses electrons

Step 3: Definition - Loss of electrons = OXIDATION

Answer: Sulfur is OXIDIZED because it loses electrons, going from an oxidation number of -2 to +4.

Part (b): Gas Volume Calculation

Question

When a sample of C₂H₅OH was combusted, the volume of CO₂(g) produced was 18.0 L when measured at 21.7°C and 1.03 atm. Determine the number of moles of CO₂(g) that was produced.

How to Approach

Use the Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT

Given: - P = 1.03 atm - V = 18.0 L - T = 21.7°C = 294.85 K (must convert to Kelvin!) - R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) - Find: n (moles)

Solution

Step 1: Convert temperature to Kelvin T = 21.7 + 273.15 = 294.85 K ≈ 295 K

Step 2: Rearrange PV = nRT to solve for n n = PV / RT

Step 3: Plug in values n = (1.03 atm)(18.0 L) / [(0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K))(295 K)] n = 18.54 / 24.22 n = 0.766 mol CO₂

Key Concepts

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT - Always convert temperature to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15 - Use R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) when P is in atm and V is in L - Alternative R = 8.314 L·kPa/(mol·K) when P is in kPa

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

A sample of nitrogen gas occupies 25.0 L at 25°C and 0.95 atm. How many moles of N₂ are present?

Check Yourself

Approach:

Use PV = nRT, convert temperature to Kelvin

Solution:

Given: - P = 0.95 atm - V = 25.0 L
- T = 25°C = 298 K - R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)

Calculate: n = PV / RT n = (0.95)(25.0) / [(0.0821)(298)] n = 23.75 / 24.47 n = 0.97 mol N₂

Key Point: Always check that your answer makes sense. At STP (0°C, 1 atm), 1 mole = 22.4 L. Our conditions are close to STP but slightly warmer and lower pressure, so ~1 mole for ~25 L makes sense!

Part (c): Volume of Ethanol Calculation

Question

Determine the volume of C₂H₅OH(l), in mL, that was combusted to produce the volume of CO₂(g) collected in part (b). (The density of C₂H₅OH(l) is 0.79 g/mL.)

How to Approach

Step 1: From part (b), we know 0.766 mol CO₂ was produced

Step 2: Use stoichiometry from the balanced equation - 1 mol C₂H₅OH → 2 mol CO₂ - Mole ratio: 1:2

Step 3: Calculate moles of ethanol moles C₂H₅OH = 0.766 mol CO₂ × (1 mol C₂H₅OH / 2 mol CO₂)

Step 4: Convert moles to mass using molar mass - Molar mass of C₂H₅OH = 2(12.01) + 6(1.008) + 16.00 = 46.07 g/mol

Step 5: Convert mass to volume using density - Density = mass/volume, so volume = mass/density

Solution

Step 1: Moles of ethanol moles C₂H₅OH = 0.766 × (1/2) = 0.383 mol

Step 2: Mass of ethanol mass = 0.383 mol × 46.07 g/mol = 17.6 g

Step 3: Volume of ethanol volume = mass / density = 17.6 g / 0.79 g/mL = 22.3 mL

Or approximately: 22 mL or 0.022 L

Key Concepts

Stoichiometry Road Map:

Quantity A → Moles A → Moles B → Quantity B
           (÷MM or ×d)  (ratio)   (×MM or ÷d)

Density Relationships: - Density = mass / volume - Mass = density × volume - Volume = mass / density

How many moles of O₂ are consumed?

Solution:

Calculate: moles O₂ = 2.50 mol C₃H₈ × (5 mol O₂ / 1 mol C₃H₈) moles O₂ = 12.5 mol O₂

Part (d): Heat Released Calculation

Question

Determine the amount of heat, in kJ, that is released by the combustion reaction.

How to Approach

Given: ΔH°comb = -1270 kJ/molrxn

This means: 1 mole of ethanol releases 1270 kJ when combusted

From part (c): 0.383 mol of ethanol was combusted

Step: Use q = n × ΔH where n = moles and ΔH = enthalpy change per mole

Solution

q = moles × ΔH°comb q = 0.383 mol × (-1270 kJ/mol) q = -486 kJ

The magnitude of heat released = 486 kJ (or ~490 kJ)

(Negative sign indicates heat is released = exothermic)

Key Concepts

Thermochemistry: - ΔH < 0 (negative) = exothermic (releases heat) - ΔH > 0 (positive) = endothermic (absorbs heat) - q = n × ΔH (heat = moles × enthalpy change per mole)

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

The combustion of methane has ΔH° = -890 kJ/mol. How much heat is released when 3.5 moles of CH₄ burn completely?

Check Yourself

Solution:

q = n × ΔH° q = 3.5 mol × (-890 kJ/mol) q = -3115 kJ

Heat released = 3115 kJ (or 3.1 × 10³ kJ)

The negative sign tells us heat is released (exothermic), which makes sense for combustion reactions!

Part (e): Temperature Change in Closed Room

Question

The combustion reaction occurred in a closed room containing 5.56×10³ g of air originally at 21.7°C. Assume that all of the heat produced by the reaction was absorbed by the air (specific heat = 1.005 J/(g·°C)) in the room.

Determine the final temperature of the air in the room after the combustion.

How to Approach

Step 1: From part (d), we know q = 486 kJ = 486,000 J of heat released

Step 2: Use the specific heat equation: q = mcΔT - q = heat (J) - m = mass (g)
- c = specific heat [J/(g·°C)] - ΔT = change in temperature (°C)

Step 3: Solve for ΔT, then find final temperature

Solution

Step 1: Rearrange q = mcΔT ΔT = q / (mc)

Step 2: Plug in values ΔT = 486,000 J / [(5.56 × 10³ g)(1.005 J/(g·°C))] ΔT = 486,000 / 5,588 ΔT = 87.0°C

Step 3: Find final temperature T_final = T_initial + ΔT T_final = 21.7°C + 87.0°C T_final = 108.7°C or ~109°C or ~110°C

Key Concepts

Specific Heat Formula: q = mcΔT - Always match units (J with J/(g·°C), or kJ with kJ/(g·°C)) - ΔT = T_final - T_initial - Positive q = temperature increases - Negative q = temperature decreases

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

A 250 g sample of water at 20°C absorbs 12,550 J of heat. What is the final temperature? (c_water = 4.18 J/(g·°C))

Check Yourself

Solution:

Step 1: Find ΔT ΔT = q / (mc) ΔT = 12,550 J / [(250 g)(4.18 J/(g·°C))] ΔT = 12,550 / 1,045 ΔT = 12.0°C

Step 2: Find final temperature T_final = 20°C + 12.0°C = 32.0°C

Part (f): Intermolecular Forces and Boiling Point

Question

The boiling points of ethanol and dimethyl ether are provided in the following table. Identify the intermolecular force that is most responsible for the difference in boiling point. In terms of this force, explain why the boiling point of ethanol is higher than that of dimethyl ether.

Compound Boiling Point (°C)
Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) 78
Dimethyl ether (CH₃OCH₃) -24

How to Approach

Step 1: Identify the types of IMFs present in each molecule

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): - Has an -OH group - H bonded directly to O - Can form hydrogen bonds

Dimethyl ether (CH₃OCH₃): - Has oxygen but NO H bonded to O - Has H bonded to C only - Cannot form hydrogen bonds - Only dipole-dipole and London dispersion

Step 2: Compare IMF strengths Hydrogen bonding > Dipole-dipole > London dispersion

Step 3: Explain the boiling point difference

Solution

The difference in boiling point is due to HYDROGEN BONDING.

Ethanol has a higher boiling point because hydrogen is bonded to oxygen. Dimethyl ether does NOT have a hydrogen bonded to F, O, or N and therefore displays NO hydrogen bonding. Hydrogen-bonded molecules have stronger attractions and therefore higher boiling points.

Key Concepts

Intermolecular Forces (weakest to strongest): 1. London Dispersion Forces - present in ALL molecules, only IMF in nonpolar molecules 2. Dipole-Dipole - present in polar molecules 3. Hydrogen Bonding - special dipole-dipole; requires H bonded to N, O, or F

Hydrogen Bonding Requirements: - Must have H bonded directly to N, O, or F - Creates very strong IMF - Dramatically increases boiling point, melting point, viscosity

Properties Affected by IMFs: - Boiling point (stronger IMF → higher BP) - Melting point (stronger IMF → higher MP) - Vapor pressure (stronger IMF → lower VP) - Viscosity (stronger IMF → higher viscosity)

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

Explain why water (H₂O, BP = 100°C) has a much higher boiling point than hydrogen sulfide (H₂S, BP = -60°C), even though both are polar molecules with similar molar masses.

Check Yourself

Solution:

Answer: Water has a much higher boiling point because it can form hydrogen bonds, while H₂S cannot.

Explanation: - Water has H bonded directly to O (very electronegative), allowing strong hydrogen bonding between molecules - H₂S has H bonded to S, which is not electronegative enough (only N, O, F qualify) - H₂S only has dipole-dipole forces and London dispersion - Hydrogen bonding in water is much stronger than the dipole-dipole forces in H₂S - Therefore, water requires much more energy to boil, resulting in a higher boiling point

Key Insight: Hydrogen bonding ONLY occurs with H bonded to N, O, or F. Sulfur is not electronegative enough!

Part (g): Le Châtelier's Principle - Equilibrium

Question

One method of producing ethanol is by the reaction of ethene and water: C₂H₄(g) + H₂O(g) ⇌ C₂H₅OH(g) ΔH° = -45 kJ/molrxn

A chemist wants to run the reaction and maximize the amount of C₂H₅OH(g) produced. Identify TWO ways the chemist could change the reaction conditions (other than adding or removing any chemical species) to favor the formation of more product. Justify your answer.

How to Approach

Use Le Châtelier's Principle: When a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to relieve that stress.

Given: ΔH° = -45 kJ/molrxn (negative = exothermic)

Consider what changes favor products: 1. Temperature 2. Pressure 3. Volume

Solution

Method 1: DECREASE THE TEMPERATURE (or increase pressure)

The chemist could allow space for more possible compositions or decrease the pressure or lower the temperature.

Justification: Increasing the pressure would make the product side favored. Since the reaction is exothermic (based on ΔH° = -45 kJ/mol), if the chemist wants to heat the reaction, heating the reaction would also follow Le Châtelier's principle because heat would cause a shift away from the product side.

Method 2: INCREASE THE PRESSURE (or reduce volume)

Justification: Looking at the balanced equation: - Reactants: 1 mol C₂H₄ + 1 mol H₂O = 2 moles gas - Products: 1 mol C₂H₅OH = 1 mole gas

Increasing pressure favors the side with fewer moles of gas. Since products have fewer moles (1 vs 2), increasing pressure shifts equilibrium toward products.

Better Answer

Method 1: LOWER THE TEMPERATURE - The reaction is exothermic (ΔH° < 0) - Lowering temperature removes heat (a "product") - System shifts right to produce more heat and more product

Method 2: INCREASE THE PRESSURE
- Reactant side: 2 moles gas - Product side: 1 mole gas - Increasing pressure favors fewer moles - System shifts right toward products

Key Concepts

Le Châtelier's Principle - System shifts to relieve stress:

  1. Temperature Changes:
  2. Endothermic (ΔH > 0): ↑ temp favors products, ↓ temp favors reactants

  3. Exothermic (ΔH < 0): ↓ temp favors products, ↑ temp favors reactants

  4. Pressure/Volume Changes:

  5. ↑ pressure (or ↓ volume) → shifts to side with fewer gas moles

  6. ↓ pressure (or ↑ volume) → shifts to side with more gas moles

  7. Concentration Changes:

  8. Add reactant → shifts right (toward products)
  9. Remove product → shifts right (toward products)

Try It Now!

Practice Question:

For the reaction: N₂(g) + 3 H₂(g) ⇌ 2 NH₃(g) ΔH° = -92 kJ/mol

Identify TWO ways to favor the formation of more NH₃.

Check Yourself

Solution:

Method 1: DECREASE THE TEMPERATURE - Reaction is exothermic (ΔH° < 0) - Lowering temperature favors the exothermic direction (forward) - Shifts toward NH₃ production

Method 2: INCREASE THE PRESSURE - Reactants: 1 + 3 = 4 moles gas - Products: 2 moles gas - Higher pressure favors fewer moles (products) - Shifts toward NH₃ production

Alternative Method: REMOVE NH₃ (though the question said "other than adding or removing species") - Removing product shifts equilibrium right to replace it

Key Application: This is the Haber process for industrial ammonia production - it uses high pressure and moderate temperature (not too high, to favor exothermic direction, but not too low, or reaction is too slow)!
