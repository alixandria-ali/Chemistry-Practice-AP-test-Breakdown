# Question 4: Molecular Geometry - Ammonia (NH₃)

## Unit: Unit 2 - Molecular and Ionic Compound Structure and Properties
**Topic**: VSEPR Theory and Molecular Geometry (SAP-4.A)

---

## Original Question
Describe the characteristics of a molecule of ammonia (NH₃). The Lewis structure and table of electronegativities are given.

```
    ••
H — N — H
    |
    H
```

The bond polarities are: [nonpolar / polar]
The molecular shape is: [bent / linear / tetrahedral / trigonal planar / trigonal pyramidal]
And the molecule is: [nonpolar / polar]

---

## How to Approach This Question

## PART A: Bond Polarity

### Step 1: Compare Electronegativities
- **Nitrogen (N)**: electronegativity ≈ 3.0
- **Hydrogen (H)**: electronegativity ≈ 2.1
- **Difference**: 3.0 - 2.1 = 0.9

### Step 2: Determine Bond Polarity
- ΔEN > 0.4 → bond is **polar**
- The N-H bond has a difference of 0.9 → **POLAR**
- N is more electronegative → partial negative charge (δ-)
- H is less electronegative → partial positive charge (δ+)

**Answer: The bond polarities are POLAR**

---

## PART B: Molecular Shape

### Step 1: Draw/Identify the Lewis Structure
```
    •• (lone pair)
    |
H — N — H
    |
    H
```

### Step 2: Count Electron Groups Around Central Atom (N)
- 3 N-H bonds = 3 bonding groups
- 1 lone pair = 1 nonbonding group
- **Total = 4 electron groups**

### Step 3: Determine Electron Geometry
4 electron groups → **tetrahedral electron geometry**

### Step 4: Determine Molecular Shape
- Molecular shape considers only atoms (ignores lone pairs)
- With 4 electron groups and 1 lone pair → **trigonal pyramidal**

Think of it as:
- Start with tetrahedral (4 groups)
- Remove one corner for the lone pair
- Leaves a pyramid with 3 atoms at the base

**Answer: The molecular shape is TRIGONAL PYRAMIDAL**

---

## PART C: Molecular Polarity

### Step 1: Consider Bond Polarities
All three N-H bonds are polar (from Part A)

### Step 2: Consider Molecular Geometry
Trigonal pyramidal is **NOT symmetrical**
- The lone pair pushes down on the bonding pairs
- Creates an asymmetric shape

### Step 3: Determine If Dipoles Cancel
- Three N-H bond dipoles point toward N
- Asymmetric geometry → dipoles do NOT cancel
- Net dipole moment points toward N (up through the lone pair)

**Answer: The molecule is POLAR**

---

## Key Concepts to Remember

### VSEPR Theory Steps:
1. Draw Lewis structure
2. Count electron groups (bonds + lone pairs)
3. Determine electron geometry
4. Determine molecular shape (ignore lone pairs)
5. Assess molecular polarity

### Common Molecular Shapes (4 electron groups):
| Bonding | Lone Pairs | Molecular Shape | Example | Polar? |
|---------|------------|-----------------|---------|--------|
| 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | CH₄ | No (symmetric) |
| 3 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | NH₃ | Yes |
| 2 | 2 | Bent | H₂O | Yes |

### Molecular Polarity Rules:
- Polar bonds + asymmetric shape = **Polar molecule**
- Polar bonds + symmetric shape = **Nonpolar molecule**
- Nonpolar bonds = **Nonpolar molecule**

### Feedback

**Approach:**
1. Compare electronegativities of P and H
2. Count electron groups around P
3. Determine molecular shape using VSEPR
4. Assess molecular polarity

**Analysis:**

**Bond Polarity:**
- P (electronegativity ≈ 2.1) vs. H (electronegativity ≈ 2.1)
- ΔEN ≈ 0.0 → **nonpolar bonds** (or very slightly polar)

**Molecular Shape:**
- Lewis structure: P in center, 3 H atoms, 1 lone pair
- 4 electron groups (3 bonds + 1 lone pair)
- Electron geometry: tetrahedral
- Molecular shape: **trigonal pyramidal** (same as NH₃)

**Molecular Polarity:**
- Although P-H bonds are essentially nonpolar
- The asymmetric trigonal pyramidal shape means the molecule has a slight dipole due to the lone pair
- **Weakly polar** (much less polar than NH₃)

**Correct Answers:**
- Bond polarities: **nonpolar** (or very slightly polar)
- Molecular shape: **trigonal pyramidal**
- Molecular polarity: **weakly polar** (though some sources may say nonpolar)

The key difference between NH₃ and PH₃ is that NH₃ is significantly more polar due to the greater electronegativity difference in N-H bonds compared to P-H bonds.
